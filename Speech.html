Audio Capture:

The first step involves capturing the audio signal containing the spoken words. This can be done using microphones, telephones, or other audio input devices.

Preprocessing:

The audio signal may undergo preprocessing steps such as noise reduction, normalization, and filtering to enhance the quality of the input signal and improve recognition accuracy.

Feature Extraction:

The audio signal is then converted into a sequence of feature vectors that capture relevant acoustic properties, such as frequency, amplitude, and duration. Common techniques for feature extraction include Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCCs) and spectrograms.

Acoustic Modeling:

In this step, statistical models or neural networks are trained to map the extracted acoustic features to phonetic units or sub-word units. These models learn to recognize patterns in the acoustic signal associated with different speech sounds.

Language Modeling:

Language models are used to incorporate linguistic constraints and context into the recognition process. These models estimate the likelihood of word sequences based on their occurrence in a language corpus, helping to improve recognition accuracy by considering the probability of different word combinations.

Postprocessing:

The final transcription may undergo postprocessing steps such as spell checking, punctuation insertion, and capitalization normalization to improve readability and accuracy.

Decoding:

The acoustic and language models are combined in a decoding process to generate the most likely transcription of the input speech signal. This involves searching through the space of possible word sequences and selecting the sequence with the highest probability according to the acoustic and language models.
